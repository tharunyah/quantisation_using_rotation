VECTORS

What Is a Vector, In AI Terms?

A vector is a list of numbers that encodes the meaning of something as a point in space.

  • In games: (x, y) is a 2D vector — position in the world.
  • In AI, a word like "cat" becomes a vector of 1000+ numbers that captures its meaning, context, and relationships.
  • Words with similar meaning (cat, kitten) land nearby in this space. Opposites land far apart.
  • Images, audio, and video are all turned into vectors too — it's the universal AI language.

QUANTIZATION

What Is Quantization?

Quantization = rounding numbers onto a coarser grid to save space.

  • float32 = 32 bits, ~7 decimal digits of precision
  • int8 = 8 bits, only 256 possible values — 4x smaller
  • int4 = 4 bits, 16 possible values — 8x smaller
  • Think of it like reducing a 4K photo to a 256-color GIF. Same scene, less data, some quality loss.
  • The goal: lose as little quality as possible while gaining as much compression as possible.

THE TRAP

Why Quantization Breaks Things

Rounding always loses information. The question is how much.

  • Real AI weight vectors have outlier values — a few numbers that are 10–100x larger than the rest.
  • The quantization grid must stretch to cover these outliers. Now all the small values get crammed into just a few grid slots — badly rounded.
  • In a transformer, this error propagates through every layer. Small errors compound into garbled output.
  • This is why naive int4 quantization often degrades model quality noticeably.
THE FIX

The Trick: Rotate Before You Round

The insight: outliers are the enemy. So get rid of them — by rotating.

  • A rotation is mathematically lossless — it preserves all distances and angles. Like spinning a photograph: same info, different orientation.
  • After rotating, the outlier energy gets spread evenly across all dimensions. No single value is extreme anymore.
  • Now quantize — the grid fits the data perfectly. Much less rounding error.
  • When you need the original values back, just rotate in reverse. Zero information lost from the rotation itself.

THE MATH

The Formula, Without Scary Math

v_quant = R⁻¹ · round( R · v )

R

rotation matrix (orthogonal)

R · v

rotate into nicer space

round()

quantize on the better grid

R⁻¹

rotate back to original space

  • The rotation matrix R can be random (works surprisingly well) or learned during training for best results.
  • In practice, Hadamard matrices are used — they're fast to compute and provably good at spreading outliers.
  • The overhead is tiny: one matrix multiply before and after quantization.
  • For a 4096-dim model, this single trick can recover most of the quality lost to int4 quantization.

This is the core of QuIP#, QuaRot, and SpinQuant.

REAL WORLD — LLMS

Applications: Large Language Models

  • Modern LLMs like Llama 3, Mistral, and Qwen all ship with quantized variants (GGUF, AWQ, GPTQ formats).
  • Rotation-based methods — QuaRot, QuIP#, SpinQuant — push quality to near-float32 at just 4 bits per weight.
  • SpinQuant from Meta reduced perplexity degradation by over 60% vs standard quantization at the same bit-width.
  • This is what makes a 70B model runnable on a single RTX 4090 instead of an 8-GPU server.

BACKGROUND

The Research Behind It

  • QuIP (2023): First showed that random rotation before quantization dramatically reduces error. Incoherence processing — making weights look random so no outlier can dominate.
  • QuIP# (2024): Added Hadamard transforms for speed + lookup tables for precision. Near-lossless 2-bit quantization.
  • QuaRot (2024): Rotates both weights and activations online. Works without any retraining.
  • SpinQuant (Meta, 2024): Learns the optimal rotation matrix end-to-end. State-of-the-art at 4-bit.

rotate → quantize → unrotate

The deeper insight: all quantization error comes from outliers. Rotation eliminates outliers without losing any information. Everything else follows.

Key papers: QuIP (Chee et al. 2023), QuIP# (Tseng et al. 2024), QuaRot (Ashkboos et al. 2024), SpinQuant (Liu et al. 2024)
